Big Tech moves $120bn AI data-centre debt off books

Category: AI

Big tech groups have increasingly financed AI-focused data centres using structures that shift roughly $120bn of related debt off their balance sheets, according to the Financial Times [1]. Separately, TechCrunch argues that 2025 was the year data centres moved from a background utility to a headline concern, as the AI boom made power, land, and build-outs central to strategy discussions [3]. Together, the coverage highlights a fast-growing AI infrastructure buildout that is creating new ways to fund capacity—while also pushing data-centre economics and constraints into the spotlight for the broader tech ecosystem [1][3].

Key Points

  • Financing shift: The FT describes companies using deal structures that can keep large chunks of data-centre borrowing off corporate balance sheets, changing how investors and lenders may assess leverage and risk in AI infrastructure expansion [1].
  • New bottlenecks: TechCrunch says the AI wave has made data-centre constraints—especially electricity access and related infrastructure—board-level issues rather than back-office logistics [3].
  • Industry spotlight: TechCrunch frames data centres as newly “center stage,” reflecting how AI demand has elevated the sector’s visibility with policymakers, utilities, and local communities that host new facilities [3].
  • Investor reading: The FT’s focus on off-balance-sheet debt underscores that understanding AI “capacity” now requires reading footnotes and partner arrangements, not just headline capex or reported net debt [1].

Technical Details

  • Off-balance-sheet financing: A set of funding structures where obligations tied to a project may not appear as debt on the sponsor’s balance sheet, depending on control and accounting rules—highlighted by the FT in the context of AI data-centre buildouts [1].
  • AI data centre: A data-centre facility built or upgraded to host large-scale AI compute; TechCrunch describes these facilities as newly central to tech strategy in 2025 [3].

Industry Impact

  • Utilities and grids: TechCrunch notes that power availability has become a defining constraint, pulling electric utilities and grid planning into the center of AI expansion discussions [3].
  • Real estate and construction: As data centres become more prominent, site selection, permitting, and construction timelines increasingly shape how quickly AI capacity can come online, according to TechCrunch’s framing of 2025’s shift [3].
  • Capital markets: The FT reports that tech groups are using financing approaches that shift data-centre debt off balance sheets, creating more deal flow for lenders and structured-finance players while complicating straightforward comparisons of corporate leverage [1].

Historical Background

Data centres traditionally sat “behind the scenes” for most consumer tech, but the rise of modern AI has increased demand for specialized compute and the facilities that house it [3].
