Hubble images giant chaotic protoplanetary disk “Dracula’s Chivito”

Category: Science

NASA’s Hubble Space Telescope has captured visible-light images of the largest protoplanetary disk yet seen around a young star, revealing a surprisingly turbulent “planet nursery” nicknamed “Dracula’s Chivito” [4][3]. The disk—officially designated IRAS 23077+6707—shows wispy material extending unusually far above and below the disk plane, with long filaments appearing more prominently on one side [4][3]. Researchers say the observation adds a fresh (and visually striking) data point for understanding how planets can form in extreme, messy environments; the findings were published in The Astrophysical Journal [4][2].

Primary Source: View original article

Did you know: Planet-forming disks are sometimes called “planet nurseries” because they are the structures from which solar systems are built [2].

Key Points

  • Sandwich scale: Popular Science reports the disk sits about 1,000 light-years away and spans roughly 400 billion miles, which NASA estimates is about 40 times the diameter of our solar system [2].
  • Heavy ingredients: The disk’s estimated mass is about 10–30 times that of Jupiter, making it not just large in size but also unusually massive for a planet-forming disk [2].
  • One-sided filaments: Astronomers note filament-like features show up on only one side, a clue that dynamic events—such as recent infalls of gas and dust—may be shaping the system [2].
  • Visible-light first: NASA and Phys.org emphasize this is the first time Hubble has shown the disk’s chaotic structure in visible light, expanding what researchers can infer beyond infrared-only views [4][3].
  • Solar system analog: Researchers suggest the volatile structure could be a “scaled-up version of our early solar system,” offering a new comparison case for theories of how planetary systems assemble [2].

Technical Details

  • Protoplanetary disk: A rotating disk of gas and dust around a young star that supplies the raw material for forming planets; the observed object is identified as such a disk around IRAS 23077+6707 [4][3].
  • Visible-light imaging: Observing in visible wavelengths can reveal scattered light from dust structures; in this case, Hubble’s visible-light view highlights unexpectedly turbulent features and extended wisps [4][3].
  • Asymmetric filaments: Filamentary structures appearing more strongly on only one side of the disk suggest uneven, time-variable shaping processes, such as recent infall of material onto the disk [2].

Industry Impact

  • Telescope synergy: The discovery underscores how visible-light imaging from Hubble can complement other observations of planet-forming disks, helping astronomers prioritize dramatic targets for deeper study with additional facilities [4][3].
  • Science communication: The playful nickname and striking morphology help bring complex disk physics to wider audiences, potentially boosting interest in astronomy and mission science outputs [2].

Scientific Significance

  • Scientific meaning: By resolving an extreme, turbulent disk in visible light, the observations broaden the known range of planet-forming environments and give theorists a rare “stress test” case for models that usually focus on more orderly disks [4][3].
  • Uncertainty and confidence: The sources describe sizes, distance, and mass estimates, but they do not provide error bars or formal confidence intervals in the reporting; readers should treat the quoted values as best estimates pending further follow-up and independent measurements [2].
  • Limitations and future work: Because the reporting highlights unusual asymmetry and possible recent infall, scientists will likely need multiwavelength observations and time on other telescopes to determine what drives the one-sided filaments and how (or whether) planets can efficiently form in such a chaotic disk [2][4].

Historical Background

Protoplanetary disks are widely recognized as the birthplace environments where planets form around young stars, as dust and gas gradually clump into larger bodies [common]. Hubble has a long history of imaging star formation and disk environments from orbit, benefiting from an unobscured view above Earth’s atmosphere [common]. This new observation adds an extreme example—large, massive, and visibly turbulent—that researchers can compare with more typical, calmer disks to test planet-formation ideas [4][3].

Q: Why might one side of a disk show longer filaments than the other?
