Anna’s Archive claims 300TB Spotify scrape, torrents planned

Category: Technology

A group calling itself Anna’s Archive says it scraped Spotify at scale, compiling nearly 300TB of data that includes audio for about 86 million songs and metadata for roughly 256 million tracks, and it says it plans to distribute the material publicly via torrents in stages [2][6][4][8]. The group frames the effort as a “preservation archive” and says the audio set covers 99.6% of listening activity on Spotify, even though it represents only a portion of the full catalog [6][3][5]. Spotify says it identified and disabled accounts involved in what it calls unlawful scraping, and says it has added safeguards and is monitoring for suspicious behavior [2][1][5][4]. The episode spotlights a tricky tech-and-culture tension: the same large-scale data tools that can enable research and archiving can also be used in ways that threaten creators’ rights and platform security [6][5].

Did you know: In its metadata analysis, Anna’s Archive says Electronic/Dance is the most track-heavy genre and that 120 BPM is the most common tempo across the dataset it compiled [10].

Key Points

  • Metadata first: Anna’s Archive has already made a torrent available that focuses on Spotify metadata—covering 99.9% of about 256 million tracks—while indicating the audio files will follow later [10][8].
  • Size breakdown: Sources describe the dump as metadata for about 256 million tracks plus audio for about 86 million songs, totaling just under 300TB and spanning about 15.43 million artists and 58.6 million albums [6][5].
  • Popularity sorting: The group says it organized releases by popularity, using Spotify’s popularity metrics to prioritize what it downloaded and plans to share [4][6][3].
  • Listening skew: In its own analysis of the scraped dataset, Anna’s Archive claims the top three songs outperformed the bottom 100 million tracks combined, illustrating how concentrated streaming attention can be [3].
  • Stated mission: Anna’s Archive—better known as an open-source search engine for shadow libraries focused on books and papers—says its broader goal is “preserving humanity’s knowledge and culture,” and it argues music should be included too [6][5].

Perspectives

Anna’s Archive: The group says it scraped Spotify to build a music “preservation archive,” intends to make files available for download, and argues that existing preservation efforts over-focus on the most popular music and very high-fidelity formats [6].

Spotify: Spotify says the activity was unlawful scraping, that it disabled the accounts involved, and that it has implemented additional safeguards while monitoring for suspicious behavior [2][4][5].

Artists and rights holders (as described by Spotify): Spotify says it stands with the artist community against piracy and is working with industry partners to protect creators and defend their rights [5].

Technical Details

  • Scraping at scale: Anna’s Archive says it discovered a method to scrape Spotify “at scale,” enabling bulk collection of track metadata and audio rather than one-at-a-time downloads [6][3].
  • Metadata vs. audio: The project distinguishes between a massive metadata dataset (track listings, related info) and a separate collection of actual music files; the metadata torrent is available first, with audio planned for later staged release [8][10].
  • Torrent distribution: Multiple reports say the archive is being shared via torrents, a peer-to-peer method that can distribute very large datasets without relying on a single hosting provider [4][7].

Industry Impact

  • Platform security: Spotify says it implemented new safeguards and is monitoring for suspicious behavior, highlighting how consumer platforms increasingly treat anti-abuse and anti-scraping as a continuous security program [5][4].
  • Digital preservation debate: Anna’s Archive’s framing of the scrape as cultural preservation underscores an ongoing debate over how to preserve digital culture when access is mediated by platforms and licenses rather than owned copies [6][5].
  • Music analytics ecosystem: Reports describe the scraped dataset as an unusually large, publicly available music metadata database, which—if widely mirrored—could influence third-party music analysis and recommendation research (even as its distribution raises legal concerns) [5][6].

Historical Background

Online “shadow libraries” and torrent distribution have long enabled large-scale sharing of copyrighted media, periodically prompting enforcement actions and technical countermeasures by platforms and rights holders [common]. Music streaming services typically deliver audio via controlled apps and licensed catalogs rather than user-downloadable libraries, which makes large-scale scraping a recurring security and anti-piracy concern for the industry [common].

Q&A

Q: What data elements are included in the metadata torrent besides track names?

A: Coverage describes metadata including items like album art, song titles, and artist names, packaged separately from the audio files [10].

Q: How complete is the audio set compared with Spotify’s full catalog?

A: Reports say the archive includes audio for about 86 million songs, which one source characterizes as roughly 37% of Spotify’s catalog, even as the group claims it covers nearly all listening activity [3][10].

Q: What does Spotify say it did immediately after detecting the scraping?

A: Spotify says it identified the accounts involved and disabled them, and it says it implemented additional safeguards to deter similar activity [2][1][5].

Action Items

  • Lock down your Spotify account: Change your password and enable available account security features (e.g., sign out of all devices) since Spotify says it disabled accounts involved in scraping and is monitoring suspicious behavior [2][4].
  • Support artists directly: If you discover favorite artists through streaming, consider buying official merch or concert tickets—legal, direct revenue streams that complement streaming royalties [common].
