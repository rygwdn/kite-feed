B.C. reopens Highway 3 detour after washout

Category: Canada

British Columbia has reopened Highway 3 to traffic between Hope and Princeton after heavy rain and atmospheric rivers washed out part of the road earlier this month [1][3][5][4]. The province restored access ahead of earlier estimates, giving holiday travelers and local communities a timely connection between the Lower Mainland and the Interior [1][4]. For now, the reopened section uses a temporary detour with traffic control, and officials are asking drivers to plan for slower travel and follow on-site directions [1][4].

Primary Source: View original article

Did you know: The Pattullo Bridge replacement project cited in coverage carries a price tag of about $1.6 billion [1].

Key Points

  • Washout details: The Dec. 16 damage included a roughly 50-metre section of roadway washed away east of the Highway 3/5 junction area [1][4].
  • Work pace: Transportation officials credited round-the-clock contractor work and available materials for reopening sooner than first expected [1].
  • Safety measures: The detour’s posted speed limit is 30 km/h, and westbound drivers may be held back to prevent lineups in an avalanche-risk zone where stopping is not allowed [1].
  • Commercial routing: The province urged commercial vehicles to use alternate routes until the detour can support two-way traffic, which officials said they expect in the new year [1].
  • Another milestone: Metro Vancouver drivers also saw a holiday-season milestone as a northbound lane opened on the new stal̕əw̓asəm (Riverview) Bridge, part of the $1.6 billion Pattullo Bridge replacement project [1].

Perspectives

B.C. Ministry of Transportation and Infrastructure: The province said it reopened the corridor between Hope and Princeton “just in time for the holidays,” while emphasizing ongoing traffic control and reduced speeds through the detour area.

Local transportation officials (Lower Mainland District): District messaging highlighted that a single lane is now open at the washout site and advised truckers to keep using alternate routes until two-way detour operations return.

  • Dec. 16, 2025: Heavy rain washed out Highway 3 section east of junction [1][4].
  • Christmas Day: Temporary detour opened with alternating single-lane traffic [1][4].

Technical Details

  • Alternating single-lane traffic: Traffic controllers (and/or signals) take turns moving vehicles in each direction when only one lane is usable, which helps keep a damaged corridor open while repairs continue [1][4].
  • Temporary detour: A short reroute built around the washout allows vehicles to pass the damaged area at reduced speeds while crews work toward restoring full two-lane capacity [1][4].
  • Atmospheric river: A long, narrow band of concentrated water vapour that can deliver intense rainfall when it makes landfall, increasing flood and washout risk in steep terrain [3][5].

Industry Impact

  • Freight and logistics: With commercial vehicles urged onto alternate routes for now, carriers may face longer trip times and scheduling adjustments until two-way detour capacity returns [1].
  • Regional tourism: Reopening ahead of schedule supports holiday-period travel between the Lower Mainland and Interior destinations, which can help small businesses that depend on seasonal visitors [4][3].
  • Construction and infrastructure: The Pattullo replacement project’s new-bridge lane opening signals phased commissioning of major assets, which can gradually improve reliability for cross-river commuting and goods movement in Metro Vancouver [1].

Historical Background

Highway 3 (the Crowsnest Highway) is one of B.C.’s key east–west routes linking the Lower Mainland to the Interior and onward connections toward Alberta, making it an important alternative when other corridors face winter disruptions [3][5]. In December 2025, successive storms brought heavy rain and atmospheric-river conditions that triggered washouts and forced closures, reflecting how vulnerable mountain transportation routes can be during extreme weather events [1][3].

Q&A

Q: Where exactly is the washout relative to major junctions?

A: Officials said it occurred about 42 kilometres east of the Highway 3/5 junction [4].

Q: Why might westbound drivers be temporarily held before the detour?

A: Officials said they may hold westbound traffic to prevent lineups in an avalanche-risk area where vehicles are not allowed to stop [1].

Q: What other route did officials mention for Lower Mainland–Interior travel during disruptions?

A: Officials noted drivers can also travel via the Coquihalla Highway, though it has had closures and delays during recent winter weather [1].

Action Items

  • Check current highway conditions before driving Highway 3: Use DriveBC and follow on-site traffic control through the detour area, since speeds are reduced and alternating traffic is in place [1][4].
  • Choose the best route for your vehicle type: If you’re driving a commercial vehicle, follow the province’s advice to use alternate routes until two-way detour travel is restored in the new year [1].
