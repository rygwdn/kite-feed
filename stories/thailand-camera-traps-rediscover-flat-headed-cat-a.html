Thailand camera traps rediscover flat-headed cat after 30 years

Category: Science

Conservation officials and researchers say Thailand has recorded the endangered flat-headed cat for the first time since 1995, using camera traps in the country’s south [1][2][4][3]. The sightings came from surveys led by Thailand’s Department of National Parks, Wildlife, and Plant Conservation and the wild cat NGO Panthera Thailand, offering fresh evidence that wetland and swamp-forest habitats can still support rare predators [1][4][3]. Reports describe the rediscovery as encouraging for conservation planning because the species is difficult to study and had been considered “possibly extinct” in Thailand [4].

Did you know: Nautilus reports flat-headed cats are only about half the size of a house cat yet act as apex predators in their wetland ecosystems [3].

Key Points

  • Repeated detections: The camera-trap program documented the species 13 times in 2024 and 16 times in 2025, according to the groups involved [3].
  • Breeding evidence: Images included a mother with cubs, which the reporting says indicates a reproducing population in the surveyed area [3].
  • Small-cat biology: Flat-headed cats have webbed toes and are adapted to wetlands and peat swamps, where they feed on aquatic prey such as fish and shrimp [3].
  • Rarity context: Articles cite an estimated total population of about 2,500 adult flat-headed cats across their Southeast Asian range [4].
  • Why hard to spot: The species is described as small, nocturnal, and associated with hard-to-access habitats like peat-swamp forests, marshes, and riverine forests, making field study challenging [4].

Scientific Significance

  • Biodiversity signal: Repeated detections suggest parts of southern Thailand still support intact wetland food webs capable of sustaining a small apex predator, which can guide habitat-focused conservation priorities [3].
  • Evidence strength: The finding is based on multiple camera-trap records across two years rather than a single anecdotal sighting, increasing confidence that the species persists locally [3][2].
  • Next research steps: Because the species is described as difficult to study due to nocturnal behavior and inaccessible habitats, continued systematic monitoring is likely needed to estimate population size and distribution within Thailand [4].

Historical Background

Thailand’s last recorded detection of a flat-headed cat was reported as occurring in 1995, after which the animal went unrecorded in the country for nearly three decades [4][3]. Nautilus notes that the long gap in records contributed to the species being classified as Endangered by the IUCN in 2008 [3]. The new camera-trap detections build on that history by adding contemporary evidence that at least some suitable wetland habitat remains for the species in Thailand’s south [3].
