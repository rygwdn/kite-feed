Freezing rain and blizzards trigger warnings across Canada

Category: Canada

Wintry hazards are stretching across Canada, with freezing rain and snow affecting parts of southern Ontario and raising the risk of slick roads and local disruptions, while Newfoundland braces for blizzard conditions in some areas [3][5][6]. In southwestern Ontario, ice has brought down trees and left many customers without electricity following a freezing-rain event [1][7]. In Quebec, forecasters are also warning that prolonged freezing rain could coat Montreal and may lead to power outages, as tens of thousands of homes were already without power after strong winds [2][4].

Did you know: Ice pellets (sleet) form when melted snow refreezes into small, hard pellets before reaching the ground, and they were included in Montreal’s freezing-rain statement [4][common].

Key Points

  • Warning levels: Environment Canada’s yellow storm warnings indicate likely localized, lower-impact conditions, while some Newfoundland alerts were upgraded to orange to reflect multiple severe hazards and potentially longer disruptions [5].
  • Extreme cold: Environment Canada also flagged extreme cold for northern parts of Alberta, British Columbia, and the Yukon alongside the broader storm pattern [5].
  • Forecast mix: Some areas north of Toronto could see ice pellets mixed with snowfall totals of 5 to 12 centimetres, adding to tricky driving conditions [3].
  • Quebec chill: Ahead of the expected icing, Montreal experienced a sharp cold snap with wind chill near −28 C on Friday morning, before warmer air is forecast to arrive [4].
  • Shopping resilience: Despite poor weather in parts of Ontario, Boxing Day still drew shoppers in major centres including Toronto, highlighting how people adapt plans around winter conditions [1].

Technical Details

  • Freezing rain: Freezing rain occurs when liquid raindrops freeze on contact with surfaces at or below 0 C, quickly creating a thin, slippery glaze that can down trees and power lines [common][1].
  • Wind chill: Wind chill reflects how wind increases heat loss from exposed skin, making it feel colder than the air temperature; Montreal was described as seeing a −28 C wind chill on Friday morning [4].
  • Weather warning colours: The Globe and Mail described yellow warnings as typically localized with minimal damage risk, while orange indicates a higher likelihood of prolonged or more disruptive severe conditions (notably referenced for parts of Newfoundland) [5].

  • Electric utilities: Downed trees and ice-related line damage can drive restoration surges for utilities and contractors, as seen in the outage reporting from southwestern Ontario and Quebec [1][2].
  • Retail timing: Boxing Day foot traffic persisted in some places despite poor weather, suggesting retailers may still see in-person demand when shoppers can safely adjust timing and routes [1].
  • Transportation: Weather-driven disruption risk rises when freezing rain overlaps with peak holiday movement, affecting road travel plans and potentially increasing demand for flexible rebooking and local alternatives [6][7].

Historical Background

Canada’s late-December period often brings rapidly shifting winter conditions as Arctic air and milder systems collide, which can produce freezing rain when a warm layer aloft melts snow before it refreezes near the ground [common]. Because freezing rain adds heavy ice to branches and power lines, it has historically been associated with downed trees and electricity outages in hard-hit events [common].

Q&A

Q: Which parts of Ontario were singled out for freezing rain versus mixed precipitation?

A: Environment Canada flagged freezing rain for the Windsor region and parts of the Greater Toronto Area, while areas north of Toronto could see ice pellets mixed with 5 to 12 cm of snow [3].

Q: Why do some warnings get upgraded from yellow to orange?

A: The Globe and Mail described orange as indicating multiple types of severe weather with disruptions that could last longer, compared with yellow warnings that are more localized and expected to cause minimal damage [5].

Q: What weather hazards were mentioned beyond snow and freezing rain?

A: The reporting noted extreme cold, strong winds, and even fog in addition to snow and freezing rain across parts of Canada [5].
