LG sets CES 2026 debut for CLOiD home robot

Category: Technology

LG Electronics says it will unveil a new domestic helper robot called CLOiD at CES 2026 in Las Vegas in early January, positioning it as part of a “Zero Labor Home” vision aimed at reducing time spent on household work [1][2][4]. The company describes CLOiD as a humanoid-style home assistant with two articulated arms and five individually actuated fingers on each hand, designed for a “wide range” of indoor chores [1][2][3]. LG also says CLOiD runs on its AI (including what it calls “Affectionate Intelligence”) and is intended to become more personalized through repeated interactions, using onboard sensors plus a camera, speaker, display, and a head-housed chipset for navigation and communication [1][2][4]. Key details remain under wraps ahead of the show, including pricing, release timing, and even full images—highlighting how CES reveals can range from near-term products to longer-term concepts [1][2][3].

Key Points

  • Dexterity design: LG says each arm has seven degrees of freedom, a design choice meant to support more natural, human-like manipulation of objects in home settings [1][4].
  • Live demos: Engadget reports that CES attendees will be able to watch CLOiD act out “real-life scenarios” at LG’s booth in the Las Vegas Convention Center [2].
  • No task list: Despite the chores framing, LG has not yet offered a specific example of a household task CLOiD can perform, according to Engadget [2].
  • Robot lab push: Android Headlines says LG has set up an HS Robotics Lab within its Home Appliance Solution division and is partnering with robotics companies in Korea and internationally to speed development [4].
  • Details withheld: LG has only shown close-up images of CLOiD’s hands so far, keeping the robot’s overall look hidden until the CES unveiling [2].

Technical Details

  • Degrees of freedom: In robotics, “degrees of freedom” describe how many independent joint motions a limb can make; LG says CLOiD’s arms have seven degrees of freedom to support flexible, human-like movement around household objects [1][4].
  • Individually actuated fingers: LG says each of CLOiD’s hands has five individually actuated fingers, which the company presents as a way to handle more delicate manipulation than simpler grippers [1][2][3].
  • Onboard perception and compute: LG says CLOiD’s “brain” (chipset) sits in its head alongside a camera, display, speaker, and sensors—components intended to help it perceive a home environment and communicate with users [1][2][4].

Industry Impact

  • Smart home platforms: A general-purpose home robot could become a new “hub” class device—pairing sensors, voice interaction, and physical manipulation—potentially reshaping how smart-home devices get orchestrated inside the home [2][1].
  • Home labor automation: LG’s “freeing customers from the time-consuming demands of housework” framing reflects a broader industry push toward robotics that targets everyday tasks, which could expand consumer expectations beyond screens and speakers to physical assistance [2].
  • Robotics partnerships: LG’s stated use of partnerships alongside its HS Robotics Lab suggests large consumer-electronics firms may increasingly combine in-house productization with external robotics innovation to speed iteration [4].
