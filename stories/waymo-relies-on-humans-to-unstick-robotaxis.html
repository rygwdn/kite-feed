Waymo relies on humans to unstick robotaxis

Category: Technology

Waymo’s self-driving robotaxis can still end up immobilized by surprisingly small issues—especially when a rider leaves a door ajar—so the company dispatches nearby tow operators via the Honk app to physically intervene and get vehicles moving again [1][2][3]. The typical “quick fix” pays about $20–$24 to shut a door (or free a snagged seat belt), while full tows for bigger problems like depleted batteries pay more [1][3]. The arrangement highlights a pragmatic, human-in-the-loop safety net as robotaxis expand: automation handles most driving, but people still step in when the real world gets messy [1][4].

Primary Source: View original article

Did you know: In some cases, tow operators free stranded robotaxis by removing seat belts that get caught in doors [3].

Key Points

  • Location friction: Some tow operators said finding a stranded robotaxi can take 10 minutes to an hour because the location provided is not always precise, sometimes requiring on-foot searching in tight streets [3].
  • Power outage strain: During a recent San Francisco power outage, retrieval requests spiked and robotaxis reportedly blocked intersections while Waymo described a “backlog” for remote human helpers who assist when traffic signals fail [3][4].
  • Pricing pushback: One San Francisco tow company manager said he declined a request because Waymo’s offered rate was below his usual $250 flatbed fee, underscoring negotiations around what ‘rescue’ work is worth [3].
  • Scaling challenge: A retired Carnegie Mellon engineering professor told the Post that paying people for door-closing and retrieval is costly and will need to shrink as Waymo grows its fleet [3].
  • Design response: Waymo is testing next-generation Zeekr vehicles in San Francisco that include automatic sliding doors, a hardware change aimed at preventing one common ‘stuck’ scenario [3].

Technical Details

  • Human-in-the-loop operations: Even with autonomous driving, fleets may rely on remote staff and field contractors to handle edge cases (like stalled vehicles or unusual road conditions), which Waymo referenced when describing a “backlog” of remote assistance during a power outage [3].
  • On-demand dispatch apps: Waymo uses an app called Honk to route paid tasks to nearby tow operators for interventions such as closing doors or towing vehicles, turning fleet support into a just-in-time logistics problem [1][4].
  • Automatic sliding doors: Waymo is testing Zeekr vehicles with automatic sliding doors, a design choice that could reduce immobilizations caused by doors being left ajar [3].

Industry Impact

  • Gig and towing work: App-dispatched “micro-rescue” jobs create a niche revenue stream for tow operators—small payments for quick fixes plus higher-fee tows—though operators dispute profitability when time-to-locate is long [1][3].
  • Robotaxi competition: The need for paid human rescue highlights an operational layer that new autonomous-taxi entrants may also need to build, affecting cost structures and service reliability comparisons with human-driven ride-hail [3][2].
  • Vehicle design priorities: Incidents tied to doors and stranded vehicles push manufacturers and fleet operators toward hardware choices (like sliding doors) and better failover planning for outages, potentially shaping future robotaxi vehicle specifications [3][4].

Historical Background

Robotaxis depend on a mix of onboard autonomy and operational support to maintain service quality at scale, a pattern common across safety-critical automation where edge cases persist [common]. The reporting illustrates a modern variant of this model: instead of in-house field teams for every issue, some interventions get outsourced on-demand to local contractors through an app-based marketplace [1][3].
