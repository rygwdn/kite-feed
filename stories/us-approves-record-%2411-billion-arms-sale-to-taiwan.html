US approves record $11 billion arms sale to Taiwan

Category: World

The US State Department has approved a weapons package for Taiwan worth around $11–11.1 billion, described as the largest US arms sale to the self-governed island to date [aljazeera.com#1][japantoday.com#1][dawn.com#1]. The proposed deal, announced during President Trump’s current term, includes multiple contracts for HIMARS rocket launchers, ATACMS and other medium‑range missiles, self‑propelled howitzers, Javelin anti‑tank systems, loitering drones, and various parts and software to modernize Taiwan’s defenses [rt.com#1][theglobeandmail.com#1][dawn.com#1]. Washington says the move supports its longstanding policy of helping Taiwan maintain a “credible defensive capability” and strengthen asymmetric warfare tactics aimed at deterring attacks, which US and Taiwanese officials argue underpins regional peace and stability [dawn.com#1][rt.com#1][washingtonpost.com#1]. Taiwan’s government, facing increasing military pressure and drills from Beijing, welcomed the announcement as part of broader efforts to boost defence spending and “safeguard peace through strength,” while China condemned the sale and urged the US to stop arming what it sees as its own territory [dawn.com#1][lemonde.fr#1][france24.com#1][washingtonpost.com#1].

Did you know: The high‑mobility artillery rocket systems heading to Taiwan are similar to those the US sent to Ukraine earlier in the decade, where they gained attention for their long‑range precision strikes [theglobeandmail.com#1].

Key Points

  • Package composition: The eight separate sales cover 82 HIMARS launchers, about 420 ATACMS missiles, roughly 60 self‑propelled howitzers, Javelin anti‑tank weapons, Altius loitering munitions, and assorted parts and software, with rocket and howitzer systems together accounting for more than $8 billion of the value [rt.com#1][theglobeandmail.com#1][dawn.com#1].
  • Procedural stage: The deal sits at the congressional notification stage, meaning US lawmakers now have an opportunity to review, block, or modify individual components before any contracts are finalized and deliveries arranged [rt.com#1][dawn.com#1][washingtonpost.com#1].
  • Taiwan’s wider buildup: Taiwan’s president recently proposed a supplementary defence budget of about $40 billion running from 2026 to 2030, signalling a long‑term plan to invest heavily in deterrence alongside this US package [dawn.com#1].
  • US policy framing: The Pentagon says the sales align with US national, economic and security interests by supporting Taiwan’s armed forces modernization and helping it field smaller, mobile, and relatively low‑cost systems suited for asymmetric warfare [dawn.com#1][rt.com#1].
  • Cross‑party backing: Taiwan’s defence ministry notes that arms purchases from the US generally enjoy broad, cross‑party political support within the island’s legislature, suggesting domestic approval is unlikely to be a major hurdle [dawn.com#1].

Technical Details

  • HIMARS rocket system: The high‑mobility artillery rocket system is a truck‑mounted launcher that can rapidly fire precision‑guided rockets over significant distances, giving Taiwan a mobile platform for striking targets while remaining harder to detect and destroy than fixed batteries [rt.com#1][theglobeandmail.com#1].
  • ATACMS medium‑range missiles: Army Tactical Missile Systems (ATACMS) are longer‑range missiles compatible with HIMARS that can hit high‑value targets such as airfields or command posts, expanding Taiwan’s ability to respond to threats without matching China’s larger arsenal platform‑for‑platform [rt.com#1][theglobeandmail.com#1].
  • Asymmetric warfare: Asymmetric warfare in this context refers to Taiwan focusing on smaller, mobile, and relatively low‑cost systems like drones, anti‑tank missiles, and rocket artillery designed to impose high costs on a stronger adversary rather than attempting to mirror its scale, an approach both US and Taiwanese officials highlight as key to deterrence [dawn.com#1][rt.com#1].

Historical Background

Since the late 1970s, the United States has supplied Taiwan with defensive weaponry under the Taiwan Relations Act, even after switching diplomatic recognition from Taipei to Beijing [common]. In recent years, China has increased military flights and naval activities around the island, while successive US administrations have expanded arms sales and high‑level contacts, contributing to a pattern of periodic tension spikes between Washington and Beijing over Taiwan’s status [dawn.com#1][ft.com#1].
