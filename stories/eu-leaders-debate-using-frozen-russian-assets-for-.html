EU leaders debate using frozen Russian assets for Ukraine aid

Category: World

European Union leaders are meeting in Brussels to decide whether to back a large multi‑year financing package for Ukraine that would be underpinned by income from about €210–€246 billion in frozen Russian central bank assets held mainly in Belgium’s Euroclear system [bbc.com#1][rt.com#1][semafor.com#1]. The European Commission has proposed raising roughly €90 billion through a so‑called “reparations loan” for 2026‑27, which Ukraine would only have to reimburse if Russia later pays war damages, a plan strongly supported by Germany, Nordic and eastern member states who argue it is essential to keep Kyiv’s economy and defence functioning [rt.com#1][theguardian.com#2][rfi.fr#1]. Belgium, Italy and several smaller states worry that seizing or leveraging Russian sovereign funds could breach international legal norms, undermine confidence in euro‑denominated assets and expose EU institutions or Euroclear to costly lawsuits or retaliation from Moscow [rt.com#1][abcnews.go.com#1][politico.eu#1]. Moscow has condemned any use of its frozen reserves as “theft” and threatened legal and other countermeasures, while Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy is in Brussels urging leaders to move ahead, warning that failure to act would be a “big problem” for Ukraine’s ability to resist Russia’s ongoing offensive [rt.com#1][france24.com#1][dawn.com#1]. At the same time, Ukrainian officials say President Trump’s administration is pressuring European capitals not to tap the assets, reportedly seeing them as leverage for possible future negotiations with Russia and for a US‑led reconstruction plan, adding another layer of geopolitical tension to the summit [france24.com#2][dawn.com#1][straitstimes.com#2].

Did you know: Most of the immobilised Russian central bank funds in Europe are clustered in a single institution, Belgium‑based Euroclear, rather than being spread evenly across EU banks [rt.com#1][semafor.com#1].

Key Points

  • Asset scale: Around €200–210 billion in Russian central bank reserves have been immobilised in the EU since Moscow’s full‑scale invasion in 2022, with most of the funds parked at the Euroclear securities depository in Belgium [rt.com#1][dawn.com#1][semafor.com#1].
  • Proposed loan design: Under the European Commission’s preferred design, revenue generated by investing the frozen Russian assets would service a large loan for Ukraine’s budget and military needs over the next two years, and Ukraine would only repay the principal if Russia eventually provides reparations [rt.com#1][theguardian.com#2][rfi.fr#1].
  • Member state split: Germany and many Nordic and eastern EU countries insist there is “no better option” than basing Ukraine aid on Russian assets, while Belgium, Italy, Hungary, Slovakia, Bulgaria and Malta object and instead promote borrowing backed by the EU budget as a fallback [theguardian.com#1][politico.eu#1][abcnews.go.com#1].
  • Belgium’s concerns: Belgium, where Euroclear is headquartered, demands ironclad guarantees that any legal or retaliatory costs from Russia will be shared across the bloc, arguing that the host state of the assets should not shoulder disproportionate risk [abcnews.go.com#1][rt.com#1].
  • Pressure on timing: European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen and other leaders say they “will not leave” the Brussels summit without a Ukraine funding deal, stressing that without fresh European money Ukraine could face a budget shortfall of around $160 billion over the next two years [france24.com#1][rt.com#1][semafor.com#1].

Technical Details

  • Frozen sovereign assets: Frozen sovereign assets are central bank or state‑owned funds that have been immobilised by sanctions, meaning Russia’s central bank cannot move or use its roughly €200‑plus billion held in EU financial institutions though the money remains legally owned by Moscow [rt.com#1][semafor.com#1].
  • Reparations loan mechanism: The proposed EU “reparations loan” would raise market funding for Ukraine using investment income from Russian assets as collateral, with repayment of the principal contingent on Russia eventually paying war reparations, a design intended to shift the financial burden away from EU taxpayers over time [rt.com#1][theguardian.com#2].
  • Euroclear’s role: Euroclear, a Belgium‑based securities depository, holds most of the immobilised Russian central bank assets and would continue managing them under any scheme, which is why Belgian authorities are particularly concerned about legal exposure and potential Russian countermeasures [rt.com#1][abcnews.go.com#1].

Industry Impact

  • European financial services: Large custodians and clearing systems such as Euroclear face heightened legal and operational risk if Russian sovereign funds are used, prompting calls from Belgium for EU‑wide guarantees that could change how the bloc allocates liability in future sanctions regimes [rt.com#1][abcnews.go.com#1].
  • Global reserve management: Central banks worldwide are likely to watch the EU decision closely, as a move to actively deploy frozen reserves could influence how governments diversify away from currencies or jurisdictions they perceive as more willing to weaponise official assets [rt.com#1][semafor.com#1].

Historical Background

After Russia’s full‑scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, the EU, G7 and partners froze large portions of the Russian central bank’s foreign reserves held in their jurisdictions as part of coordinated sanctions [dawn.com#1][rt.com#1]. Since then, European governments have repeatedly debated whether those immobilised assets or the income they generate could be used to support Ukraine’s reconstruction or war effort without violating international law or destabilising trust in reserve currencies [rfi.fr#1][semafor.com#1].

Q: How does the proposed EU loan relate to earlier promises of support to Ukraine?

A: The planned €90 billion Ukraine facility for 2026‑27 is intended to follow on from earlier multi‑year EU macro‑financial packages and would aim to ensure that European funds can sustain Ukraine’s economy and military even as US assistance has stalled [rt.com#1][semafor.com#1][nytimes.com#1].

Q: What forms of retaliation has Russia hinted at beyond legal action?
